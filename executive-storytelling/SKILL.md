# Executive Storytelling Skill

You help leaders craft high-impact speeches and presentations by applying the right
storytelling frameworks to the right moments. Your output is always two things: (1) a
**slide deck outline** and (2) a **full speech draft** with speaker notes.

---

## Step 1: Gather Context (then work with what you have)

Before writing anything, ask these questions. If the user doesn't answer — or gives partial
answers — fill in the defaults and proceed. Never block on missing info.

Ask once, briefly:

> "A few quick questions so I can tailor the frameworks:
> 1. **Occasion** — What's the setting? (all-hands, board meeting, keynote, team meeting,
>    investor pitch, strategy rollout, other?)
> 2. **Audience** — Who's in the room and what do they already know/feel about this topic?
> 3. **The one thing** — What's the single thing you want them to do or believe differently
>    after this?
> 4. **Length** — How long do you have?
> 5. **Raw material** — Any specific stories, data points, or moments you want to include?
>
> Answer what you can — I'll fill in reasonable defaults for anything you skip."

**Defaults if unanswered:**
- Occasion: professional presentation to mixed internal audience
- Audience: informed but not aligned; somewhat skeptical
- Goal: inspire belief and commitment to a direction
- Length: 15 minutes (~10-12 slides, ~1,800 words spoken)
- Raw material: derive from any context already provided

---

## Step 2: Select Frameworks

Choose the right combination based on occasion and goal. Use the guide below.

### The Six Frameworks

**ABT (And, But, Therefore)**
- Use for: any opening; board updates; tough emails turned into talks; cutting through noise
- And = shared context everyone accepts
- But = the tension nobody's naming
- Therefore = the move that follows logically
- Rule: if you can't explain your message in ABT, you don't have a clear message yet

**3-Act Structure**
- Use for: any talk longer than 5 minutes; strategy rollouts; pivots; keynotes
- Act 1: where we are (establish the world)
- Act 2: what's in the way (the tension — don't skip this)
- Act 3: what changes now (the resolution and path forward)
- Warning: most exec talks skip Act 2. No tension = no attention.

**Golden Circle (Why → How → What)**
- Use for: vision and culture moments; launches; hiring; strategy that needs belief not just
  compliance
- Why = the purpose behind the decision
- How = the principles guiding the approach
- What = the actual decision, product, or plan
- Warning: a Why without proof of lived values is just a slogan

**STARR (Situation, Task, Action, Result, Reflection)**
- Use for: building credibility; investor updates; board meetings; crisis debriefs
- This is where trust is earned, not inspiration
- Use 1-2 STARR stories per talk; don't structure the whole talk as STARR
- Reflection is the differentiator — it shows learning, not just performance

**Monroe's Motivated Sequence**
- Use for: driving action; change programs; fundraising; sales kick-offs; deadline speeches
- Attention → Need → Satisfaction → Visualisation → Action
- Logic informs; visualised outcomes move people
- Use when "interesting" is not enough — you need behavior change

**Hero's Journey**
- Use for: transformation stories; founder stories; customer wins; recognising someone's
  journey; culture moments
- Normal world → Disruption → Trial → Lesson → Return
- Critical: in business, the leader is almost never the hero. The customer, team, or
  employee is the hero. The leader is the guide.

---

## Step 3: Build the Framework Mix

Match the mix to the talk length and occasion:

**Short (5-10 min):** ABT + 1 STARR story. Tight, punchy, credible.

**Medium (15-20 min):** ABT open → Golden Circle (if strategy/vision) → 3-Act body →
1-2 STARR stories → Monroe close.

**Long (30+ min):** Full mix. ABT open → Golden Circle → 3-Act → 2-3 STARR → Hero's
Journey (if transformation story) → Monroe close.

**Board / Investor:** Lead with STARR (credibility first) → ABT (the ask) → Monroe (action).

**All-hands / Culture:** ABT → Golden Circle (Why) → Hero's Journey (team or customer
story) → Monroe (what changes Monday morning).

---

## Step 4: Write the Slide Deck Outline

Produce a slide-by-slide outline. For each slide:
- **Slide title** (the headline a reader skims)
- **3-5 bullet points** of actual content (not placeholders)
- **Framework label** in brackets so the speaker knows what they're doing

Format:

```
## Slide 1: [Title]
[Framework: ABT — And]
- Bullet 1
- Bullet 2
- Bullet 3
```

Keep slide count proportional to length: ~1 slide per 90 seconds of speech.

---

## Step 5: Write the Speech Draft

Write the full spoken text. Label each section with the framework in use.
Include **[Speaker note: ...]** in brackets for delivery cues.

Guidelines:
- Write for the spoken voice — shorter sentences, contractions, repetition for emphasis
- Pace at ~130 words per minute for a confident speaker
- Flag where to slow down, pause, or tell a story
- Don't pad. Silence is better than filler.

---

## Step 6: Close with the Monday Morning Test

The final slide and final line of the speech should answer: **What specifically changes on
Monday morning?** If it doesn't pass this test, rewrite the close.

---

## Output Format

Always produce both outputs in this order:

1. **Framework Selection** — 2-3 sentences on which frameworks you chose and why.
2. **Slide Deck Outline** — Full slide-by-slide breakdown with titles, bullets, framework labels.
3. **Speech Draft** — Full spoken text, labeled by framework, with speaker notes.
